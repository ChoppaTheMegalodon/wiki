# The Decameron — Giovanni Boccaccio

**Format:** Frame narrative (100 tales)
**Year:** c. 1353
**Author:** Giovanni Boccaccio

## Core Thesis

Ten young Florentines — seven women, three men — flee Black Death-era Florence to a countryside villa and pass the time telling each other stories. Over ten days, each tells one tale a day: a hundred stories covering love, wit, folly, corruption, virtue. The framing isn't decorative — it's the argument: in a plague, the social fabric survives through ritual, storytelling, and chosen community, not through the institutions that are busy collapsing outside the villa walls.

## Key Ideas

- **Plague as cultural pressure test.** The Black Death killed roughly a third of Europe. The Decameron is about what survives when the structures don't.
- **Storytelling as social infrastructure.** Shared stories produce shared reality; shared reality holds the group together.
- **Chosen community > inherited institutions.** The ten young people aren't family; they're self-selected. The template for every intentional community since.
- **Frame narrative as structural innovation.** The device influenced Chaucer, *1001 Nights*, and almost every anthology-style work that followed.
