## Core Thesis

Grant programs in token networks fail for predictable reasons: no clear thesis, unclear selection criteria, no feedback loop, no graduation path. Good grant programs work because they're designed as part of a community machine, not as a standalone bucket of money. The money is the least important variable — the structure around it is what produces outcomes.

## Key Ideas

- **Start with a thesis, not a budget.** "We want to fund X kinds of work because we believe Y." Without the thesis, every grant becomes ad-hoc and community members can't self-sort.
- **Tiered structure.** Small frictionless grants for proof-of-interest; medium grants for validated ideas; large grants for proven contributors. Linear tiers create a progression that filters naturally.
- **Feedback loops matter more than outcomes on any individual grant.** The lessons from declined/failed/shipped grants, surfaced publicly, become the community's collective training data.
- **Graduation paths.** The best grantees should graduate into roles, partnerships, or independent projects. Without a path out, grants become rent-seeking.
- **Public, auditable, opinionated.** Hidden grant decisions erode trust faster than bad grant decisions.

## Where This Shows Up in My Work

- [[../pyth/programs#impact-awards|Impact Awards]] — the tiered, community-judged, publicly-auditable structure is a direct descendant of this essay's framework.
- [[../pyth/programs#pythentity|Pythentity]] — the "graduation path" idea maps onto the identity layer. Contributors who graduate through Impact Awards accumulate on-chain reputation via Pythentity, which unlocks access to larger programs.
- The 2M PYTH Clipping Incentive Program (in planning) uses the tiered-grants thinking directly — small unrestricted clip rewards, medium project grants, large creator deals.

## Where I Disagree / Would Extend

[TBD — early:
- The essay focuses on grant program *design* but doesn't spend enough on grant program *sunset*. When should a grants program shut down? How do you know when the community has outgrown it?
- Underweights the political / governance layer. Grant programs often become factional battlegrounds — who decides, how, with what transparency. The essay assumes these decisions resolve cleanly; in practice they rarely do.
- The "feedback loop" idea is right but hard — who does the writing, under what incentive? If the grants team writes the post-mortems, they'll self-serve. Third-party post-mortems are rare in crypto. Worth a separate piece on who audits the auditors.]
