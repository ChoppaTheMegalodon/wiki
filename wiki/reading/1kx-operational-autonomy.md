## Core Thesis

Token network communities don't start autonomous — they start founder-dependent. Operational autonomy is a maturation arc, not a state to be declared. Communities progress from (1) founder-run, through (2) founder-supervised with community execution, to (3) community-led with founder advisory, to (4) fully autonomous. Most protocols get stuck at stage 2 forever because the founders can't let go and the community hasn't accumulated the coordination capacity to take over.

## Key Ideas

- **Autonomy is a process, not an event.** Announcing a "decentralized community" on day one doesn't make it so. The capacity has to be built, and the building takes years.
- **Founder-dependence is the default — and fine, at the start.** The problem isn't early centralization; it's refusing to hand off as the community's capacity grows.
- **The hand-off is the hard part.** Founders have to actively transfer decision rights, budgets, and narrative authority. Silence on hand-off is read as permission to escalate founder dependence forever.
- **Coordination infrastructure matters.** Governance forums, multisig structures, working groups, grant processes — these are the scaffolding that lets autonomy actually function. Without them, "autonomy" is theater.
- **Narrative control is the last thing to hand over.** Budgets and decisions can go before the ability to speak for the protocol does. Many communities get operational autonomy but not narrative autonomy, and the mismatch causes drift.

## Where I Disagree / Would Extend

[TBD — early:
- The essay presents autonomy as almost always desirable. Sometimes it isn't — some communities work better with benevolent founder-dependence, especially in the early phases, and pushing for autonomy prematurely breaks them.
- Underweights the cultural layer. Operational autonomy without cultural autonomy produces mercenary communities that can execute but can't defend values. Both have to mature together.
- The essay is optimistic about "coordination infrastructure" — forums, multisigs, working groups. In practice this infrastructure also creates new power structures, some of which capture value rather than distribute it.]
