# BD Philosophy

How I approach partnerships, integrations, creator deals, and protocol-to-protocol relationships at Pyth. The frameworks below aren't theoretical — they're what I actually run every outreach and evaluation through.

---

## Partnership Tiers

Not every relationship needs the same weight. The tiers force clarity about what's actually being exchanged:

| Tier | Counterparty Commitment | What We Provide |
|------|------------------------|-----------------|
| **Integration** | Uses Pyth data feeds | Listed in ecosystem, basic co-marketing, technical support |
| **Strategic** | Deep product alignment | Joint hackathons, shared grants, co-development resources |
| **Exclusive** | Category exclusivity | Major co-marketing, governance coordination, first-mover positioning |

Most partnerships should sit at Integration. Strategic is a significant resource commitment from both sides — reserve it. Exclusive is rare and should require real mutual value (not just "we like you"). Getting the tier right prevents the most common BD mistake: over-promising on low-commitment deals and burning the relationship when you can't deliver.

## The Six Principles

### 1. Inversion Thinking

Before every partnership, I ask: **"What would make this fail?"** Then avoid those things.

It sounds obvious but almost nobody does it. The standard BD approach is to imagine the upside and pitch that. Inversion flips it — you assume the partnership is already broken six months from now, work backward to figure out why, and patch the holes before the handshake.

Most BD failures are predictable. Mismatched timelines, mismatched audiences, mismatched stakes. Name them out loud in the first meeting. If both sides can't address the failure modes, the partnership isn't ready.

### 2. The Trojan Horse

**Embed value before asking for anything.**

Ship a demo, write their case study, build the integration spec first. Show up to the call with something already half-built. Cold pitches with no prior value-add have a ~2% hit rate. Warm pitches with an artifact ("I noticed you're doing X — I built this for you, it took two hours, do you want it?") have a ~40% hit rate. Same message, twenty times the conversion, because the artifact does the trust-building the email can't.

This is how the best BD at Pyth runs. We don't pitch. We ship.

### 3. Counter-Positioning

Every BD conversation has a dominant alternative — the thing the counterparty is already doing. Counter-positioning makes that alternative the liability:

> "You're paying Bloomberg $X/month for delayed data. We're free, faster, and on-chain. The only reason not to switch is inertia."

Say it out loud. Don't dance around it. If the counterparty already knows the problem, naming it is clarifying; if they didn't, naming it is educational. Either way, you've reframed the default from "keep paying Bloomberg" to "defend why you're still paying Bloomberg." That's a different conversation.

### 4. Community as Social Proof

**"500 builders already using this"** beats any pitch deck.

Community metrics are the only unfalsifiable BD asset. Anyone can claim partnerships. Anyone can claim TVL (sort of). Nobody can fake a living, breathing, culturally coherent community that shows up every day. Lead every BD conversation with that — it changes the entire negotiation posture.

### 5. The Uncomfortable Question

Cold outreach almost always opens with a pitch. That's why cold outreach doesn't work.

Open with **a genuine question the counterparty can't easily answer** — one that suggests you've thought about their business harder than they have:

> "How are you handling weekend price discovery on your perps product? Because I've looked at a few of your competitors and they all have the same problem."

Now they have to respond. They have to engage with the question. And you've started the conversation with them doing the work of answering, which is a completely different dynamic than them evaluating a pitch.

### 6. Meme-First GTM

**If the community can't meme it, the partnership isn't culturally sticky.**

This sounds glib but it's the most practically useful BD filter I have. A partnership that the Discord can riff on, that produces a running joke, that generates reaction images — that's a partnership people remember. A partnership that needs a polished announcement tweet and dies the next day — that's a press release.

The test I run internally: can my best meme-creators make something funny about this deal in the first week? If no, the cultural stick rate is zero. The deal might still make sense commercially, but don't expect the community to carry it.

## Community as the BD Asset

The through-line across every principle above: **the community itself is the primary BD instrument.**

- 500K members = social proof.
- Active contributors = case studies.
- Meme-literate Discord = cultural stickiness.
- Verified identity layer ([Pythentity](programs#pythentity)) = credibility across incentive programs.
- Content stack = machine for turning any partnership into 100+ pieces of derivative content.

When a partner signs with Pyth, they don't just get data feeds — they get access to a distribution and proof-generation engine that most protocols can't replicate at any price. That's the real pitch. Everything else is detail.
