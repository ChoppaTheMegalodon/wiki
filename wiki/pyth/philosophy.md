# Community Philosophy

The theoretical backbone for everything I do at Pyth. Communities are living systems with their own physics — energy flows, immune responses, cultural DNA, decay patterns. They are a culture. Treat them mechanistically and they die. Treat them as organisms and they compound. 

Most of what follows I worked out across a lifetime of being terminally online and engaged in countless online communities for the past 2 decades. My employment in Community for the past 5 years has culminated in essays which attempt to outline what i have learned about community and how to apply it at scale. The theory here matters because community and storytelling (eschatology) drives all media. This is not yet realised widely. Knowing where the puck is headed tells you what to ship and what to kill.

---

## Communities Are Living Machines

The default mental model for "community" in most protocols is a marketing funnel. Get people to show up, farm their attention, convert it into token velocity. We know (because of Crypto twitter and web3 for the past 5 years) that this is unsustainable. It doesn't work.

The real model is one we implicitly know, but don't often articulate. A community is a living machine with:

- **White blood cells** — long-term contributors who defend the culture when it's under attack. They carry the values. Protect them ruthlessly.
- **Energy vampires** — people who extract attention and status without contributing. Identify them early; isolate them before they drain the contributors around them.
- **Crabs in the bucket** — the ones who pull down anyone who tries to climb. A fatal pattern if tolerated. See [Crabs in a Bucket](../writings/Crabs%20in%20a%20Bucket;%20a%20Crypto%20Seafood%20Boil/Crabs-in-a-Bucket-a-Crypto-Seafood-Boil) for the full mechanic.
- **0→1 builders** — the fuel of the flywheel. They're not your current top contributors, they're the *next* ones. Spend time here generously.

Every program, incentive, moderation decision, and partnership move I make at Pyth is filtered through this framing. Do these decisions feed the white blood cells or starve them? Do builders or extractors get rewarded? Does it make the immune system stronger or weaker?

## The Flywheel

This is how i think about the community flwheel compounding:

```
    Recognize → Reward → Attract → Identity → Contribute ─┐
          ↑                                                 │
          └─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

1. **Recognize.** Real contributors publicly, by name, specifically. Be open.
2. **Reward.** Tokens, reputation, access, status. Whatever maps to what their actual wants are.
3. **Attract.** Recognition + reward is visible, which appeals to a potential new cohort.
4. **Identity.** New contributors have opportunities to attach their identity to the network. They stop being "users" and become "Pythians."
5. **Contribute.** Identity drives ownership, ownership drives contribution.
6. Repeat. Every rotation makes the next easier.

Most brands skip steps 1 and 4. They go straight from rewards to contribute, and wonder why they get mercenary farmers instead of communities. Recognition and identity are not optional. They are the load-bearing scaffolding to your brand cathedral.

The full theory is in [Why Communities Beat VCs](../writings/why-communities-beat-vcs/why-communities-beat-vcs). Everything I ship at Pyth is designed to feed at least one step in this cycle.

## The Community Calculus

A decision framework. Before I commit to any initiative:

1. **Does this strengthen the flywheel?** If no, don't do it.
2. **Who does the work? Who benefits? Are incentives aligned?** If the people doing the work don't benefit, it's exploitation with extra steps. Kill it.
3. **What's the minimum viable version?** If it can't ship in a week, it's overcomplicated. Reduce.
4. **Will this matter in 12 months?** If no, deprioritize ruthlessly. Most things don't.
5. **Does this create dependencies or remove them?** Prefer removal. Every new dependency is a future blocker.
6. **The Cynicism Audit.** What would a smart skeptic say about this? If you can't defend against that, it's not ready.
7. **The Hope Extraction.** After the cynicism, is there still a reason to believe? That's the real signal. Conviction that survives skepticism is the only kind worth building on.

This framework kills more ideas than it greenlights. That's the point. Most ideas are worse than they seem at first glance. The ones that survive all seven gates tend to be winners.

## The 'Pub Test'

If someone at the pub doesn't understand your pitch, your program, or your value proposition, it probably sucks.

Complexity & Jargon is not depth or quality. Every piece of community-facing communication must be easily explained to someone 3 pints deep at your local watering hole.

If you can't, the idea isn't complete yet.

## Slop is the Enemy

The modern attention economy runs on *Slop.* Algorithm-optimized slop only reinforces echo chambers. Trading cards, reply bots, engagement farms, AI-generated thread-spam. It's corrosive to your soul (real community). Slop trains people to be pavlovian doom scrolling attention machines using cheap dopamine instead of making room for substantive, meaningful contributions.

The antidote is **clarity + authenticity**. Recognize good (authentic) work publicly with specifics. Call out low-effort slop privately with care. Never beef. A community capable of handling the truth is the one that grows. The one that can't is a suicide cult.

The real audience you should be building for aren't mercenary or transitory. **They are creatives who have agency. Builders.** The people plugged in enough to recognise they are exhausted by slop and are looking for a rabbit hole to fall into, not dopamine traps. High-potential individuals who need an avenue & constructive honesty to move from 1 to 100.

## Cultural Monuments, Not Liquidations

Most communities end in total liquidation. You hit TGE & the exchange listings drop. Everyone gets their airdrop. That whole playbook is designed to maximize energy at a single point of explosion, and everything after it is managing the fallout.

It is totally backwards. The goal should be building **cultural monuments.** Communities can outlast individuals running them, outlast current market cycles, outlast tech stacks. Fundamentally, real community has to think in decades, not quarters.

If you're designing for liquidation, you're in the wrong game. The community people want is the one that still exists in 2035, is still making memes, still has a heartbeat, is still capable of recognizing itself. The only way to build that is to refuse the shortcuts. Culture requires care.

---

## Systems are NOT campaigns

**Campaigns have an endpoint. Systems grow and compound**

Every initiative in the Pyth community is evaluated on its ability to add or detract from the system. A Twitter campaign for a launch is a campaign. A recurring delivery pipeline that the community feeds into week after week is a system. It's obvious which one will still be producing value in 18 months.

This is why Pyth programs (in [[programs]]) look the way they do — MissionMonitor, Pythentity, the Content Incentive Stack. It's not one off events or extraction events, they're architecture. Build once, run forever.
