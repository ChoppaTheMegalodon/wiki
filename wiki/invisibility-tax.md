# The Invisibility Tax

> The hidden career cost of letting only your boss know you're good at your job.

## The Diagnosis

Most professionals are structurally underpriced relative to their actual skill. Not because they're bad at their work — because **no one outside their org chart knows it**. Their boss knows. Maybe a few colleagues. That's it. And when the only person aware of your value is the person with the incentive to keep paying you less, you will get paid less and promoted less. That's the tax.

You pay this tax silently for your entire career if you don't change the dynamic.

## Why It's Worse Now

The old social contract — head down, loyalty, annual pay rises, wage growth compounding into a middle-class life — **is broken**, particularly in Australia and the broader Western world.

- Wage growth is flat in real terms.
- Cost of housing has run away.
- Promotions are slower, fewer, and increasingly require visible external demand for you.
- Employers default to the underpriced asset unless forced otherwise.

Meanwhile, the people who **do** make noise — post, publish, build public reputations — benefit from a winner-take-most dynamic driven by internet distribution. The gap between invisible and visible widens every year.

## The Cultural Obstacle (Australia)

Self-promotion is culturally uncomfortable, especially in Australia where tall-poppy instinct still lingers. The people around you — family, friends, colleagues — often have a gravity that pulls visible contributors back down. "Don't be a wanker" is the default posture.

The counter-argument: *if the people in your life aren't happy that you're building, those might be the wrong people.* Unrelated detail, but load-bearing — you can't opt out of this tax if your social circle punishes the move.

Tall-poppy pressure is fading because the broken social contract has forced younger workers to realize **no one is coming to save them**. The cultural window is opening.

## The Fix

Stop paying the tax by becoming legible to the market outside your current employer. Three moves:

1. **Build a portfolio** — not just work samples; proof of how you think. See [[portfolio-as-proof]].
2. **Own a narrow niche** — aggregate news and ideas around one specific domain. Become "the person who cares most about X."
3. **Be authentically yourself in public** — the more human your voice, the more you stand out in an AI-saturated feed. Jokes, takes, blurred work/life — this is the edge, not the liability.

## The Paradox

Counter-intuitively, the *more* personal and informal you are, the *more* employable you become. LinkedIn culture assumes professionalism = stiff corporate voice; the actual market rewards the opposite. The people "standing out like dog's balls" are the ones winning roles, clients, and deals.

This paradox gets stronger as AI content floods every platform. When 90% of the feed is generated slop, authentic human voice becomes scarcer — and scarcity is value.

## The Smallest First Move

Charlie's prescription (Gearside, [[src-stop-being-invisible]]):

1. Register a real domain (not a Wix/Squarespace subdomain — "act like an adult")
2. Build a simple site — Claude or ChatGPT can do it in an afternoon
3. Do the first post. This is the hardest threshold.

Once the first post is out, the delta drops. The friction is almost entirely between zero and one.
